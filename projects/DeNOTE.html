DeNOTE

DeNOTE is a social note-sharing platform made for my First Year Team Project.

Concept

DeNOTE was developed during my first year at The University of Manchester, for COMP 10120, in which we had to develop a data-oriented web application.

As university students constantly sharing lecture notes through apps like Messenger or Whatsapp, we felt that we could develop a more practical way to do this.

It's true that similar websites exist, but these hide the majority of documents behind a paywall. We also wanted to add a social aspect to our product which other websites lack. If you know one of your friends creates great study material, you could follow them on DeNOTE to have easy access to their notes. If you had any questions about a document, you could simply ask them in the comments section. Need to access notes offline? Simply download them for later use.

Development

While everyone in the team worked in both the front- and backend, we decided to split the group into two. As I was a Human Computer Interaction student, I had a lot of responsibilities in the front-end department, alongside some back-end and non development tasks.

Let's first talk about the backend. Built using mySQL, SQL, and PHP, our database was used to store notes in PDF and image formats, as well as storing user profiles. A lot of care was used when designing the tables we'd use, as we knew that every design decision could have many consequences further in development.

To develop an accessible, intuitive, and modern front-end we used Bootstrap alongside custom JavaScript. After prototyping the flow and design of the website, development of our frontend went smoothly. Building a mobile accessible website was essential, as we believed that's how most students would use it: in the bus before an exam. In the end, our efforts paid off as we were voted the "application with the Most Appealing User Interface" by the other students.

Of course, a project of this scope didn't only involve development tasks. I was also in charge of legal considerations, such as Terms and Conditions, and planning how to handle copyright claims and user reports. I was also often in charge of handling complaints within the group about other team members and task distribution.

The last part of development was used for polishing the product, in which I refined the UI and fixed a potential SQL injection exploit.

UI

One of my main motivations in pursuing a career in Human Computer Interaction was due to my interest in accessibility. Because of this, a lot of care was given to the UI and the UX.

We wanted DeNOTE to work on any device regardless of viewport size. It wasn't a mobile-first nor a desktop-first approach, but a mix of both. This is why we chose to use Bootstrap, which made developing a responsive website easy and fast.

To make our website sleek and modern we looked at how other websites had solved the same issues we had, but applying our own twist to it. For example, the visual card organisation in the subjects page was inspired by how image thumbnails are presented in an Instagram profile.

After developing mockups, we started to design our own branding. As this was a tool meant for students in our university, the colour branding is heavily based in the purple of The University of Manchester. The fonts Montserrat and Lato were chosen because of how they paired together and their easy legibility at all sizes.

Finally, the name DeNOTE is a stylized mesh of "The Note", inspired by the verb "to denote", to stand for, to make known. From there, the tagline "sharing DeNOTEs you need" was an easy play on words to come up with.
